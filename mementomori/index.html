Memento Mori Memento Mori（记得你会死）

Because Enkidu my friend is turned to clay, I too shall lie down and not rise again, ever. I am afraid of death, and so I roam the wilderness seeking the secret of life. The Epic of Gilgamesh (c. 2100 BCE) 只因我的挚友恩奇都已化作泥土，我也终将躺下，永不再起。我畏惧死亡，于是流浪于旷野，去寻那永生的秘密。 《吉尔伽美什》（约公元前 2100 年）
Man is a god who shits — a being half symbolic self able to imagine the infinite, half perishable body destined to feed worms, and unable to bear the seam between them. Ernest Becker, The Denial of Death (1973), paraphrased
人是一个会拉屎的神——一半是能够想象无限的、象征性的自我，一半是终将喂养蛆虫的、会腐烂的肉身，而无法承受这两者之间的那道接缝。
Man is a god who shits — a being half symbolic self able to imagine the infinite, half perishable body destined to feed worms, and unable to bear the seam between them. 欧内斯特·贝克尔，《拒斥死亡》（The Denial of Death，1973），意译

To be alive is to be finite, and most of what a life is spent doing turns out, on inspection, to be ways of not looking at that. Memento mori — remember that you will die — is the oldest instruction to look anyway; what the looking then demands is the question everything that follows turns on. The intuition behind it has two parts, only one of which survives scrutiny.

The first part is the temptation to dramatize — to feel that finitude has been done to us, that behind the bare fact of dying there sits a malice, or at least a meaning. There is neither. Biological death is not aimed at anyone; it has no author and no address. This is the recognition Camus arrives at in The Myth of Sisyphus [3], where the universe is found to be not hostile but simply silent, indifferent, going about its business. To read a hidden intention into mortality is to force a shape onto something that has none, and the forcing is itself a way of not looking — only dressed now in the language of tragedy rather than the language of distraction. Set the drama aside and what remains is plainer and harder: mortality is a condition, the simple fact of being finite, with no tribunal behind it and no verdict in it, only a limit.

The second part is the one with a literature behind it. An enormous share of ordinary life functions to keep that limit out of view, and this is no longer a poetic grievance but a thesis, argued most forcefully by Ernest Becker [1]. His claim is that the fear of death is the root drive behind human behaviour, more fundamental than Freudian sexuality or Adlerian power. The human being, on his account, is split down the middle: a symbolic self that can picture eternity, housed in a body that excretes and decays and will rot, and the gap between the two is unbearable to look at directly. So it is covered over, and the covering is culture itself. What Becker calls the immortality project — religion, nation, art, children, vocation, wealth — is the machinery by which a person comes to feel they have outlasted their own body in some symbolic register. We do not, on this view, act mainly in order to live; we act in order to belong to something more durable than ourselves. The countless things we busy ourselves with are not incidental distractions from the fact of death. They are the structural work of forgetting it.

Heidegger had already described the same machinery in another vocabulary. The default condition of human existence he calls Verfallen, falling [2]: a flight into chatter, curiosity, and busyness that quietly hands death over to das Man, the anonymous "one." One dies, of course — eventually, in general, as a fact about everybody and therefore about nobody in particular — and so the actual self is excused from facing it just now. Mortality is admitted precisely in the form that lets it be evaded: as a truth about the species rather than an appointment of one's own. Where Heidegger keeps a door open onto an authentic alternative, Becker is the darker of the two, doubting that the psyche can bear a fully lucid look at death at all, and reading certain breakdowns — the deep depressions, the derealizations — as what happens when the denial fails.

The thesis has an obvious vulnerability. By crowning a single drive as the explanation of everything, Becker courts exactly the reductionism he accuses Freud of, one key filed to open every lock. Its empirical heir is Terror Management Theory [8], which has run several hundred mortality-salience experiments and gathered real support, though it now sits inside psychology's replication crisis and the sturdiness of some of its signature effects is in question. It is a deep and far-reaching hypothesis rather than a settled finding — true enough, perhaps, to organize everything that follows, not so settled as to end the argument.

活着就意味着有限，而一个人一生所做的大半事情，细看之下，其实都是不去看这件事的种种方式。Memento mori——记得你会死——是要人偏偏去看的那道最古老的训令；而看过之后，它究竟要求什么，正是此后一切所系的问题。这道训令背后的直觉里藏着两样东西，唯有其中一样经得起推敲。

第一样，是把它戏剧化的诱惑——觉得有限是被强加到我们身上的，觉得在「人会死」这一光秃秃的事实背后，端坐着某种恶意，或者至少是某种意义。两者都没有。生物学意义上的死亡并不针对任何人，它既无作者，也无收件地址。这正是加缪在《西西弗神话》（The Myth of Sisyphus）里抵达的那个认识[3]：宇宙并非怀着敌意，而只是沉默、漠然，自顾自地运转。把一重隐藏的意图读进必死之中，是向那本无形状之物强加一个形状，而这一强加本身就是一种不去看，只是这一回，它说的是悲剧的语言，而非分神的语言。把戏剧搁在一旁，剩下的东西更素朴、也更难：必死是一种条件，是「有限」这一简单的事实，它背后没有法庭，里面没有判决，有的只是一道界限。

第二样，才是身后有一整片文献撑着的那一样。日常生活中极大的一部分，所发挥的功能正是把那道界限挡在视线之外；这已不再是一句诗意的牢骚，而是一个论题，由欧内斯特·贝克尔陈述得最为有力[1]。他的主张是：对死亡的恐惧，是人类一切行为背后的根本驱力，比弗洛伊德的性、比阿德勒的权力都更为根本。依他之见，人是从中间裂开的：一个能描画永恒的象征性自我，住在一具会排泄、会衰败、终将腐烂的身体里，而这两者之间的裂缝，无法被直视。于是它被遮盖起来，而那遮盖物，就是文化本身。贝克尔所说的不朽工程（immortality project）——宗教、国族、艺术、子嗣、事业、财富——正是一个人借以在某个象征的层面上感到自己已经活过了肉身的那套机器。依此而论，我们行动，主要不是为了活着，而是为了归属于某种比自身更经久的东西。我们忙忙碌碌的那无数事情，并不是对死亡这一事实的偶然分神，而是「遗忘死亡」这桩工程的结构性劳作。

海德格尔早已用另一套词汇描述过同一台机器。他把人之存在的默认状态称为沉沦（Verfallen）[2]：逃入闲谈、好奇与忙碌之中，悄悄把死亡交付给「常人」（das Man），那个无名的「人们」。「人」当然是要死的：终归会死，笼统地死，是一桩关于每一个人、因而关于谁也不是的事实；于是真正的那个自我便被宽免于此刻面对。必死之所以被承认，恰恰是以那种容许它被回避的形式：作为关于这一物种的真理，而非一桩属于自己的约期。海德格尔为一种本真的出路留了一道门，而贝克尔是两人中更幽暗的一个，他怀疑心灵究竟能否承受对死亡彻底清醒的一瞥，并把某些崩解，例如深度的抑郁、现实感的丧失，读作否认机制失灵时所发生的事。

这一论题有一处明显的弱点。贝克尔把单一的驱力加冕为万事的解释，正好招来了他指责弗洛伊德的那种化约论：一把被锉得能开尽所有锁的钥匙。它的实证后裔是恐惧管理理论（Terror Management Theory）[8]，已开展数百个「死亡显著性」实验，积累了真实的支持，然而它如今正坐在心理学的可复制性危机之中，其若干招牌效应的稳固程度都成了问题。它是一个深刻而影响深远的假说，而非一项已成定论的发现；或许真到足以组织起此后的一切，却又没有坐实到足以让争辩就此止息。

I · Being-toward-Death, and No Way Around It 绕不开的向死而生 · Being-toward-Death, and No Way Around It

"Caius is a man, men are mortal, therefore Caius is mortal" had always seemed to him correct as applied to Caius, but by no means to himself. That Caius was mortal was right enough; but he was not Caius, not an abstract man — he was a creature quite apart from all the others. Leo Tolstoy, The Death of Ivan Ilyich (1886), paraphrased [22]
「凯乌斯是人，凡人皆有死，故凯乌斯有死」——这条三段论，他一向觉得用在凯乌斯身上完全正确，用在自己身上却绝非如此。凯乌斯是会死的，这没错；可他并不是凯乌斯，不是抽象的人，他是一个与所有旁人全然不同的存在。
"Caius is a man, men are mortal, therefore Caius is mortal" had always seemed correct as applied to Caius, but by no means to himself — he was not Caius, not an abstract man, but a creature quite apart from all the others. 列夫·托尔斯泰，《伊凡·伊里奇之死》（The Death of Ivan Ilyich，1886），意译 （[22]）

If falling is the default, the question is whether there is anything else, and Heidegger's name for the something else is Vorlaufen — anticipation, or, more literally, running ahead. The first thing to say is what it is not, because almost everything that gets sold under the heading of "facing your mortality" is in fact the thing it is not. It is not a technique. It cannot be broken into steps. The moment one tries to turn it into a procedure, a morning practice, a checklist, it slips back into being one more object of concern — Besorgen, in his term — and falls again into the very busyness it was meant to interrupt. Its content is not a method but a structure.

So set it apart from its imitations: from thinking about death, from meditating on death, even from the memento mori held up before the eyes as a reminder. Each of those still treats death as a future event, which is exactly how the anonymous "one" treats it — the proposition that one dies eventually, a fact lodged safely in the calendar and bearing on no one in particular. Vorlaufen does something stranger: it takes death as a possibility, and holds it as such. In Heidegger's three terms, death is ownmost [2] — no one can die my death for me, and this single fact pulls me out of the anonymous crowd into being unmistakably myself. It is non-relational — in its presence every role and relation falls away, none of them can come with me across that line, and so it strips bare which of my possibilities are genuinely mine and which were only ever the crowd's. And it is not-to-be-outstripped — being the absolute end, it gathers the whole spread of my possibilities into one finite total, which is the only condition under which a life can be seen as a whole rather than endlessly postponed.

What this comes to, in the texture of an actual day, is modest and exact: a choice made in the clear knowledge that the time being spent on it is finite and unrecoverable, rather than under the soft illusion that there is always more where it came from. Under that knowledge, a choice gets its weight back. Heidegger keeps the whole analysis deliberately formal — Being and Time describes the structure of being-toward-death and pointedly refuses to prescribe how to live, on the principle that the latter is each person's own affair and that any general recipe would simply hand authenticity back to the crowd it was supposed to break with.

There is a serious objection, and it comes from Levinas [4]. To set up death as "my ownmost possibility," he argues, is a quiet form of self-absorption. What actually tears the fabric of the everyday, on his account, is not my own death but the death of the other — the mortality in the face across from me, which arrives not as a fact about my finitude but as a claim on my conduct. Read that way, Vorlaufen stays sealed inside the self, placing responsibility to others somewhere after ontology rather than before it. The disagreement is not a footnote; it marks a real and unresolved tension between an ethics that begins with the other and a more solitary, Schopenhauerian temper that begins with one's own finitude.

如果沉沦是默认，那么问题就是：除此之外还有没有别的？海德格尔给「别的」起的名字，叫先行（Vorlaufen），字面意思是向前奔赴。首先要说清楚的，是它不是什么，因为几乎一切以「直面你的必死」为名兜售的东西，其实正是它所不是的那样东西。它不是一套技术。它无法被拆成步骤。人一旦试图把它变成一道程式、一项晨间功课、一张清单，它便滑回去，重新成为又一个操劳的对象（以他的术语说，即 Besorgen），再度沉沦进它本要打断的那种忙碌里。它的内容不是一套方法，而是一个结构。

所以，把它与它的种种仿制品分开：与「想着死」分开，与冥想死亡分开，甚至与那悬在眼前、用作提醒的 memento mori 分开。这几样仍把死亡当作一桩未来的事件，而这恰恰是那无名的「常人」对待它的方式——「人终归要死」这一命题，一桩安稳地躺在日历里、与任何具体的人无涉的事实。先行所做的事更为奇异：它把死亡当作一种可能性，并如此把它持守住。以海德格尔的三个词说，死亡是最本己的[2]：没有人能替我去死，单是这一桩事实，就把我从无名的人群里拔出来，成为不容混淆的我自己。它是无所关联的：在它面前，一切角色与关系尽皆脱落，无一能陪我越过那道线，于是它剥露出，我的诸般可能性里，哪些真正属于我，哪些从来不过是人群的。它又是不可逾越的：身为那绝对的终点，它把我全部可能性的铺展收拢成一个有限的整体，而这是「一段生命得以被作为整体来看、而非被无尽地延宕」的唯一条件。

把这落到实实在在的一天的肌理里，它谦逊而精确：做出一个抉择时，清楚地知道用在它上面的时间有限而不可挽回，而不是抱着「来日方长」那种温吞的错觉。在那份清楚之下，抉择重获其重量。海德格尔把整套分析刻意保持形式化。《存在与时间》描述向死存在的结构，并断然拒绝规定该如何生活，其原则是：后者是每个人自己的事，而任何通则式的配方，都只会把本真性又交还给它本应与之决裂的那个人群。

有一个严肃的反驳来自列维纳斯[4]：他论证道，把死亡设立为「我最本己的可能性」，是一种悄然的自我专注。依他之见，真正撕裂日常之布的，不是我自己的死，而是他者之死——对面那张面容里的可朽，它来到我面前，不是作为关于我之有限的一桩事实，而是作为对我之举止的一项要求。如此读来，先行始终封闭在自我之内，把对他者的责任安置在本体论之后某处，而非之前。这一分歧不是脚注，它标记出一重真实而未决的张力：一边是以他者为起点的伦理，一边是以一己之有限为起点的、更孤独的叔本华式气质。

II · Of Past Regrets and Future Fears 敬往昔的懊悔与未来的敬畏 · Of Past Regrets and Future Fears

Ah, my Beloved, fill the Cup that clears
To-day of past Regrets and future Fears —
Why not? for Tomorrow I myself shall drink again, weighed down with a thousand years of grief. Edward FitzGerald, after Omar Khayyám, Rubáiyát (1859), as quoted in Civilization VI, here rendered back from the Chinese
啊，我的爱人，请倒满我面前的酒杯。
让我们干杯，敬往昔的懊悔与未来的敬畏。
为何不敬明日？因为，明日我会带着千年的忧愁再饮一杯。
Ah, my Belovèd, fill the Cup that clears / To-day of past Regrets and future Fears — / why not? for Tomorrow I myself shall drink again, weighed down with a thousand years of grief. 爱德华·菲茨杰拉尔德，译自欧玛尔·海亚姆，《鲁拜集》（Rubáiyát of Omar Khayyám，1859），《文明 VI》引文

At the far pole from the immortality project sits a third reply to the same condition — neither Becker's denial nor the Buddhist release, but a clear-eyed, lyric kind of acceptance. Their provenance changes how one reads them, and it is unsettled: FitzGerald's 1859 version is famously a re-creation rather than a translation [10][11], and whether it carries the mind of the eleventh-century Persian astronomer Omar Khayyám or of a Victorian Englishman writing out his own loss of faith under a Persian disguise has never been resolved. It is two scepticisms laid one over the other: the old Persian tradition of wine and roses, and the metaphysical chill that came over the educated English after Darwin.

It wears carpe diem on the surface, but its core is closer to Epicurus. The decisive turn is the refusal to honour tomorrow, and the reason given is not that tomorrow is unreal but that tomorrow one will drink it "weighed down with a thousand years of grief" — a line that quietly concedes the full depth of time and the way sorrow accumulates in it. This is not a denial of death; it is the gesture made after death has been seen whole. It builds no monument, founds no immortality project, promises no afterlife — it is cold, even faintly mocking, toward the theology that would offer one — and concludes that since immortality cannot be had, the present cup is the thing to drain.

And yet the cup has a difficulty folded into it, and the whole quarrel turns on a single question: is meaning point-like or line-like? The refusal to honour tomorrow bets that meaning can only ever be present, that every deferral is a loss, and so pours everything into the intensity of the now. The opposite wager is that meaning is irreducibly stretched across time, that it lives in promise, in narrative, in being pointed at something, and therefore cannot be packed into a single instant. And the two are not even quarrelling about the same tomorrow.

The tomorrow worth withdrawing

Epicurus is not arguing that tomorrow is unreal but about where value lives. To fear a future death is to suffer it in advance, and when it finally comes the one who feared it is no longer there to meet it — death, in his formula, "is nothing to us." Dread of tomorrow is then pure expenditure that buys nothing, and the reasonable thing is to stop paying it. This is the tomorrow worth retiring: the anxiety that pre-pays a fear, that mortgages the present to a future which never actually arrives for the one doing the worrying.

The tomorrow worth keeping

But a person is not a slice of time; a person is a story. The things most worth doing — a body of work, a long inquiry, anything built — cannot be finished inside a single present, and their meaning comes precisely from continuity across many of them. A self that lived purely in the now could not carry such a thing forward, because it would neither keep nor care about what came before. A life worth living has narrative unity, and narrative needs a future tense, a not-yet that it is for. To withdraw all regard from tomorrow is to demote a self from a story into a string of unconnected sparks. This is the tomorrow of promise and commitment — the "what it is for" lit up from the far end by being-toward-death.

The poem's ambiguity, read this way, is really its precision. It wants both at once: it waves off tomorrow even as the "thousand years of grief" admit the whole depth of time. What it shows is not a life lived in a pure present but a person who knows exactly how deep time runs and chooses, for this one cup, not to draw on it — which is as far from idle drinking as restraint is from blindness. So the two tomorrows are not the same tomorrow. The dread-laden one is what Epicurus is right to retire; the borne one, the tomorrow of promise and narrative, is what Kierkegaard and Heidegger are right to keep; and the line's small genius is to fuse the two into a single word.

Which leaves a more livable formulation than either pole on its own: honour today as today, while holding that another today will arrive tomorrow, and will be honoured the same way. This separates the two tomorrows the poem deliberately tangles, and then ties them back together. It is no longer the aesthete's frightened clutch at the now for want of anything to trust; Kierkegaard's diagnosis misses it [5], because the despair he describes comes from a lack of continuity, and here continuity is present — not in sacrificing today for tomorrow, but in a commitment renewed at each arriving present. The nearest name for it is not Epicurus, who stays static and withdrawing, but a gentled eternal recurrence [7]: Nietzsche's test asks whether one could will to live this same life again, identically, without end, and the figure who can say "once more" has reached amor fati. The formulation turns that ordeal into a rhythm — each successive present received willingly rather than endured as repetition — and asks less than Nietzsche does, since it requires affirming only the next today, not the whole chained fate. It also quietly settles the standoff between Heidegger and Epicurus: the tomorrow being honoured is neither death nor anxious pre-payment but simply the fact that there will be another today. Death stays in the background as the limit that makes the honouring matter — because the todays are finite, because one of them will at last fail to arrive, each that does arrive carries weight. Finitude is not denied; it has receded into the background and become the thing that gives the rhythm its weight rather than a blade held over it. That is Vorlaufen once it has succeeded: death assumed rather than stared at, condensed into the density of an ordinary day.

在与不朽工程相对的那一极，坐着对同一处境的第三种回答——既不是贝克尔的否认，也不是佛教的解脱，而是一种清醒的、抒情的接受。这些诗行的来历会改变人读它的方式，而它的来历并无定论：菲茨杰拉尔德 1859 年的译本，出了名地是再创作而非翻译[10][11]，它所传达的，究竟是十一世纪波斯天文学家欧玛尔·海亚姆的思想，还是一个维多利亚时代的英国人借波斯外衣写下的自身信仰失落，至今未有定谳。它是两重怀疑主义叠在一处：古老的波斯酒与玫瑰的传统，加上达尔文之后笼罩英国知识阶层的那阵形而上学的寒意。

它表面披着「及时行乐」，但其内核更接近伊壁鸠鲁。那决定性的转折，是对敬奉明日的拒绝，而所给的理由并非明日不真，而是明日饮它时将「带着千年的忧愁」——这一句悄悄承认了时间的全部纵深，以及忧愁如何在其中累积。这不是对死亡的否认，而是死亡被看个透彻之后所做的姿态。它不立纪念碑，不立不朽工程，不许来世（它对那愿意供奉来世的神学冷淡，甚至略带讥诮），并断定：既然不朽不可得，眼前这一杯才是该饮尽的东西。

然而这杯酒里裹着一重困难，整场争执系于一个问题：意义是点状的，还是线状的？拒绝敬奉明日，押注于「意义唯能在场」，押注于「每一次延宕都是流失」，于是把一切倾入当下的强度。相反的押注是：意义不可化约地横跨于时间之上，活在承诺里，活在叙事里，活在「朝向某物」里，因而无法被塞进单一的片刻。而这两者所争的，甚至不是同一个明日。

该撤回的那个明日

伊壁鸠鲁并非在论证明日不真，而是在论证价值栖于何处。畏惧未来的死，是预先就受其苦，而当它终于到来，那个畏惧它的人已不在场去迎接它——以他的话说，死「与我们无关」。如此，对明日的畏便是一笔什么也换不来的纯支出，理性的做法是停止支付。这正是该撤回的那个明日：一种预支恐惧的焦虑，它把当下抵押给一个对自己从不真正到来的未来。

该守住的那个明日

可人不是一片时间的切片，人是一个故事。那些最值得做的事——一整片著述、一桩漫长的探究、任何被建造起来的东西——都无法在单一的当下里完成，而它们的意义，恰恰来自跨越其中许多个当下的连续。一个纯粹活在当下的自我无法把这样的东西推进下去，因为它既不留存、也不在意先前之物。值得过的人生有叙事的统一，而叙事需要一个将来时，一个它所朝向的「尚未」。撤回对明日的全部顾念，便是把一个自我从一个故事降格为一串互不相干的火花。这是承诺与委身的那个明日，是那个被向死存在从远端点亮的「为了什么」。

这样读来，这首诗的含混其实正是它的精确。它两样同时都要：它挥手让明日走开，与此同时，「千年的忧愁」又承认了时间的全部纵深。它所展示的，并不是一种活在纯粹当下的人生，而是一个清楚地知道时间有多深、却选择就这一杯不去支取它的人；这与漫不经心的痛饮之别，正如克制有别于盲目。所以，这两个明日不是同一个明日。那焦虑的明日，是伊壁鸠鲁有理由撤回的；那被承担的明日，那个承诺与叙事的明日，是克尔凯郭尔与海德格尔有理由守住的；而这一句小小的天才之处，在于把二者熔进了同一个词。

由此留下一种比任何单一极端都更能安顿人的表述：以今日为今日而敬重它，同时持守着——明日会有另一个今日到来，并将以同样的方式被敬重。这把诗有意搅在一起的两个明日分开，再把它们系回去。它不再是审美者因无可信靠而对当下惊惶的攥紧；克尔凯郭尔的诊断在此落空[5]，因为他所描述的绝望来自连续性的缺失，而这里连续性是在场的：它不在「为明日牺牲今日」之中，而在「于每一个到来的当下被重新兑现的承诺」之中。它最接近的名字不是伊壁鸠鲁，后者始终静态而退缩，而是一种被温和化了的永恒轮回[7]。尼采的试验问，人能否意愿一模一样、永无止境地重过这同一生，而那能说出「再来一次」的形象，已臻于 amor fati。这一表述把那场磨难化为一段节律，每一个相继到来的当下被欣然领受，而非作为重复被忍耐，而它要求的比尼采少，因为它只需肯定下一个今日，而非整条被锁链的命运。它也悄然了结了海德格尔与伊壁鸠鲁之间的僵持：被敬重的那个明日，既非死亡，亦非焦虑的预支，而仅仅是「还会有一个今日」这一事实。死亡留在背景里，作为那道使「敬重」成其为要紧的界限：正因这些今日有限，正因其中一个终将不再到来，每一个确实到来的今日才承载着重量。有限并未被否认，它退入了背景，成为赋予这节律以重量之物，而非悬在头顶的一把刀。这便是先行成功之后的样子：死亡被承担，而非被盯视，凝缩进了寻常一天的密度里。

III · True Courage 真正的勇敢 · True Courage

I know that rashness and cowardice are both faults, and that the virtue of courage is the mean between these extremes. But it is better to err on the side of too much courage and be rash than to fall short and be cowardly. Just as prodigality is nearer to generosity than miserliness, so rashness is nearer to true courage than cowardice. Miguel de Cervantes, Don Quixote (1605–1615), paraphrased
我知道鲁莽和怯懦都是过失，勇敢的美德是这两个极端的折中。但是，宁可勇敢过头而鲁莽，也不要勇敢不足而怯懦。就像挥霍比吝啬更接近慷慨的美德，鲁莽也比怯懦更接近真正的勇敢。
I know that rashness and cowardice are both faults … but it is better to err toward too much courage and be rash than to fall short and be cowardly; just as prodigality is nearer to generosity than miserliness, so rashness is nearer to true courage than cowardice. 米格尔·德·塞万提斯，《堂吉诃德》（Don Quixote，1605–1615）

The passage opens on textbook Aristotle [13]: courage, andreia, is the mean, mesotēs, between two vices — rashness, which is too much, and cowardice, which is too little. Then it adds something Aristotle does not quite say in these words [12]: that since one is going to err toward one of the two faults anyway, one should err toward rashness, on the reasoning that prodigality stands nearer to generosity than miserliness does.

The addition is closer to Aristotle than it first looks. Aristotle held that the two extremes are not equidistant from the mean — one of them usually resembles the virtue more than the other. And his own example happens to be this one: in the matter of courage, the rash man looks more like the brave man than the coward does, because the rash man is at least leaning in the right direction and merely overshooting, while the coward has withdrawn altogether and is simply not there. So the line is not a misreading; it draws out one of Aristotle's less-noticed refinements — that the mean does not sit at the dead centre, and that virtue leans toward the fault whose direction of effort is right.

Turned toward mortality, the claim becomes sharp: in a finite life the cost of cowardice is greater than the cost of rashness. Cowardice is a kind of absence — a stretch of life not actually lived — because to hold back from acting in order to preserve a body that is going to die regardless is to forfeit the action and keep nothing that lasts. The rash at least showed up, leaned in, threw themselves at something, even if they overshot. For a being certain to lose everything in the end, "did not dare" is the more complete loss; "overdid it" at least happened.

And the line turns ironic in the mouth that speaks it: a man who takes windmills for giants and flocks of sheep for armies, who endures every indignity for a Dulcinea who does not exist. Cervantes puts a flawless piece of Aristotelian ethics into the mouth of someone whose judgment has failed, and then refuses to tell us whether a man who hurls himself at a world certain to crush him is a hero or a fool — he lets Quixote be both at once. Which is why the qualification Aristotle would insist on still stands: lean toward rashness, but keep the eye that can still tell a windmill from a giant. The hard part, the part Cervantes wrote a whole book around, is that the eye and the readiness to charge tend to vary inversely — the more clearly you see, the less you leap. The same logic closes The Myth of Sisyphus [3]: having seen the absurd — the stone always rolling back, the windmill always only a windmill — one rebels not because one expects to win but because not rebelling would be to go cowardly absent from one's only life. Quixote charging the windmill is the knight's version of Sisyphus putting his shoulder to the stone; both would rather act to excess than withdraw.

这段话的开篇是教科书式的亚里士多德[13]：勇敢，andreia，是两种恶习之间的中道，mesotēs：一边是过度的鲁莽，一边是不及的怯懦。接着它添了一样亚里士多德没有完全这样说出来的东西[12]：既然人终归要偏向两个过失中的一个，那就该偏向鲁莽，其理由是，挥霍比吝啬更近于慷慨。

这一添加，比初看更近于亚里士多德。亚里士多德认为，两个极端与中道并不等距：其中一个通常比另一个更像那美德。而他自己所举的例子，恰好就是这一个：在勇敢这件事上，鲁莽的人比怯懦的人更像勇敢的人，因为鲁莽的人至少朝着对的方向倾斜，只是用过了头，而怯懦的人则整个退了出去，根本不在场。所以这一句并非误读，它带出了亚里士多德一处较少被注意的精微：中道并不端坐在正中央，德性偏向那个用力方向正确的过失。

把它转向必死，这主张就变得锋利：在有限的一生里，怯懦的代价大于鲁莽的代价。怯懦是一种缺席，是一段并未真正活过的生命，因为「为了保全一具终归要死的身体而不去行动」，是放弃了行动，又什么经久之物都没留下。鲁莽至少到场了、倾身了、朝某样东西扑了过去，纵然用过了头。对一个终究要失去一切的存在而言，「没敢」是更彻底的损失；「做过了头」至少发生过。

而这一句一旦出自说它的人之口，便转为反讽：那是一个把风车当巨人、把成群的羊当军队、为一个并不存在的杜尔西内娅受尽屈辱的人。塞万提斯把一段无懈可击的亚里士多德伦理学，放进一个判断力已然失灵的人口中，然后拒绝裁断：那个向着必将碾碎自己的世界扑过去的人，究竟是英雄还是傻瓜。他让堂吉诃德同时是两者。这正是为什么亚里士多德必会坚持的那一限定依然成立：偏向鲁莽，但保留那只仍能分清风车与巨人的眼睛。难处（塞万提斯为之写了一整本书）在于，那只眼睛与那股扑上去的劲往往此消彼长：看得越清，越是不扑。同一逻辑收束了《西西弗神话》[3]：既已看清荒诞（石头总要滚回，风车永远只是风车），人之反抗，不是因为指望取胜，而是因为不反抗就等于怯懦地缺席于自己唯一的一生。堂吉诃德冲向风车，是骑士版的西西弗把肩膀抵上巨石；二者都宁可用力过度，也不退场。

IV · The Depreciated Legacy of Cervantes 塞万提斯被贬低的传承 · The Depreciated Legacy of Cervantes

Once, I too saw the future as the only competent judge of us. Later I understood that to flirt with the future is the worst conformism, a craven flattery of the mighty — for the future is always stronger than the present. If the future held no power in my eyes, then to whom was I attached? To God? To country? To the people? My answer was at once sincere and ridiculous: I am attached to nothing but the depreciated legacy of Cervantes. Milan Kundera, Jerusalem Prize address (1985), The Art of the Novel, paraphrased
从前，我也一样，把未来当作唯一有能力审判我们的审判者。后来我才知道，与未来调情是最低劣的因循随俗，是对最强者的懦弱奉承。因为未来总是强过现在。可是，如果未来在我眼中不具任何效力，那么我依恋的又是谁？是上帝？是祖国？还是人民？我的回答既真诚又可笑，我谁也不依恋，除了塞万提斯被贬低的传承。
Once, I too saw the future as the only competent judge of us … if the future held no power in my eyes, to whom was I attached? To God? To country? To the people? My answer was at once sincere and ridiculous: to nothing but the depreciated legacy of Cervantes. 米兰·昆德拉，耶路撒冷文学奖演说（1985），《小说的艺术》（The Art of the Novel）

Kundera says this in his own voice, not a character's, and means it as a position taken [14]. It arrives at an odd place to be loyal to — a depreciated, powerless legacy — but it gets there by deposing a judge. The judge he names is more modern and more specific than any tribunal of the cosmos: it is the future. What is at issue is not death but the imagined court of historical time, the bench that is supposed to look back someday and rule on whether the present was right.

To flirt with that future, he says, is the worst conformism and a flattery of the mighty — and the charge belongs to political philosophy, aimed squarely at the age of ideology, at the Central-European twentieth century whose working logic was that the people of the present could be spent for the sake of a future paradise, and that present cruelty would be ratified by history as just. Deference to a future that is certain to win is, on this view, the cheapest courage there is, and the gravest cowardice is to hand oneself over to it as a guaranteed-winning judge, because doing so excuses one from ever answering for the present. It is cowardice of a higher order: not the failure to act, but the surrender of one's verdict to whoever is going to win. The deflation has an older, Eastern echo: the "Won-Done Song" of Dream of the Red Chamber [20] asks where the generals and ministers of old have gone, and answers that they are now only an empty name for later men to revere — the future's esteem, the very prize one is tempted to defer to, shown to be worth nothing once one is no longer there to collect it.

Once the future is deposed as judge, the same question returns that the two tomorrows raised: if the future holds no authority, what is left to be attached to? Kundera runs through the standing candidates and rejects each — God, who is religion's future; country, the nation's future; the people, the revolution's future — which are, not coincidentally, three of the twentieth century's largest immortality projects. His answer is not a future at all but a legacy: something turned toward the past, depreciated, without power. What he is loyal to is the form Cervantes opened — the novel — understood precisely as the art that refuses to judge, that withholds the final verdict, that lets each character keep a truth of their own. That Quixote is hero and fool at once, and that Cervantes declines to rule on him, is not a failure of the book but its particular dignity, and it is this suspension of judgment that Kundera swears himself to.

So only someone who does not defer to the future can fully inhabit the present; only someone who has deposed every external judge can, attached to nothing, receive each present as it comes. "Attached to nothing" is not nihilism — the attachment is simply restricted to what does not demand that the present be redeemed by the future. What is left to hold, once every winning judge is deposed, is only the depreciated legacy of Cervantes: the novel that refuses to judge, turned toward the past, without power, paying nothing back. To be loyal to it is at once a vast freedom and a possible rootlessness, and to stand by something powerless is to accept standing across from every victor. That, in his telling, is the cost of lucidity, and also its dignity.

昆德拉以自己的声音说出这番话，而非借某个人物之口，且把它当作一个被采取的立场[14]。它最终落在一个奇怪的效忠对象上——一个被贬低、没有权力的传承——但它是经由废黜一个审判者抵达那里的。他所指认的审判者，比任何宇宙的法庭都更现代、更具体：它是未来。所争的不是死亡，而是历史时间那座想象中的法庭，那张被设想为有朝一日回头来裁断「当下是否正确」的审判席。

与那个未来调情，他说，是最低劣的因循随俗，是对最强者的奉承。这一指控属于政治哲学，矛头直指意识形态的时代，指向那个东欧的二十世纪，它的运作逻辑是：当下的人可以为了未来的天堂而被花掉，而当下的残酷将被历史追认为正当。依此而论，对一个必将得胜的未来的恭顺，是世上最廉价的勇敢，而最深的怯懦，是把自己交付给它、视之为稳赢的审判者，因为如此一来，人便永远无须为当下负责。这是一种更高一阶的怯懦：不是不去行动，而是把自己的裁断让渡给那个终将取胜的一方。这一番贬抑，在东方有一更古老的回声：《红楼梦》的〈好了歌〉（[20]）问「古来将相可还存」，又自答「也只是虚名儿与后人钦敬」——后人的钦敬，那正是人受诱去恭顺的奖赏，一旦人已不在场去领取，便被点破为一无所值。

未来一旦被废黜其审判者之位，「两个明日」所提出的那个问题便再度回来：倘若未来不具权威，还剩下什么可供依恋？昆德拉把现成的候选者逐一数过，又逐一否弃——上帝，是宗教的未来；祖国，是国族的未来；人民，是革命的未来——而这三者，并非巧合地，正是二十世纪最大的几项不朽工程。他的回答根本不是一个未来，而是一个传承：一个朝向过去、被贬低、没有权力的东西。他所效忠的，是塞万提斯开启的那个形式，即小说，并恰恰把它理解为那种拒绝审判、悬置最终裁断、让每个人物各自保有一份自己真理的艺术。堂吉诃德同时是英雄与傻瓜，而塞万提斯不予裁断，这不是这本书的失败，而是它独有的尊严，昆德拉所矢志的，正是这种审判的悬置。

于是，唯有不向未来恭顺的人，方能充分栖居于当下；唯有废黜了一切外部审判者的人，方能在无所依恋之中领受每一个到来的当下。「无所依恋」并非虚无主义：所依恋者，仅限于那不要求「以未来赎买当下」之物。当一切得胜的审判者都被废黜，所剩下、可供持守的，便只有塞万提斯被贬低的传承：那拒绝审判的小说，朝向过去，没有权力，分文不偿。效忠于它，既是一种浩大的自由，也可能是一种无根之境；而守着一件没有权力的东西，就意味着接受自己站在每一个胜利者的对面。依他的讲法，这是清醒的代价，也是它的尊严。

V · To Know the Work of Creation, Read the Tale of Deliverance 欲知造化会元功，须看西游释厄传 · To Know the Work of Creation, Read the Tale of Deliverance

Would you know the work that creation turns through its ages? Then read the Tale of Deliverance from Calamity — the Journey to the West. Closing couplet of Chapter 1, Journey to the West (16th c.)
欲知造化会元功，须看西游释厄传。 《西游记》第一回回末联（16 世纪）

Journey to the West [19] runs, beneath the magic and the slapstick, on a single hidden engine: every order of being in it — gods, immortals, demons, kings — is trying to get out from under death, and the whole hundred-chapter machine is driven by that one craving. The title couplet says as much in plain sight. To know the work of creation — how the cosmos turns through its huge Shao-Yong cycles of birth and extinction — one is told to read a Tale of Deliverance from Calamity, Xiyou shi'e, which was in fact one of the novel's early titles. The 厄, the calamity to be released from, is finally death itself; the book is a long study of the ways one tries to undo it.

It begins exactly where Becker's thesis would predict. The Monkey King is the happiest creature on his mountain, king of a paradise of fruit and waterfalls, until the day he watches an old monkey die and grasps that he too is entered in King Yama's ledger of the dead. In the middle of the feast he weeps; and the next morning he sails off alone across the ocean to find a teacher and learn the Way. The quest for deathlessness begins the instant death is first glimpsed — not from misery but from the sudden intolerable knowledge, in the midst of happiness, that it will end. Every later striving in the book is a continuation of that first flight from the ledger.

What follows is a hierarchy of immortality with each rung, in turn, knocked down. The first and crudest is stolen deathlessness, seized by force or fraud. Monkey gorges on the peaches of immortality and the elixir of the Daoist heaven, and then, summoned to the underworld, seizes the register of life and death and strikes his own name and his tribe's from it with a brush — cheating the ledger directly. It buys him centuries and a great deal of trouble, and it is exactly what gets punished: pinned for five hundred years under the Mountain of Five Phases. The flesh-eating demons of the road want the same thing by the same shortcut — to devour the pilgrim Tripitaka, whose body is rumoured to confer long life — and they are the stolen route in its appetite form. One cannot erase oneself from the ledger; the attempt is the sin.

Above theft is cultivated deathlessness, the real internal alchemy Monkey is taught on the mountain — discipline, breath, the refining of the self. The book treats it as genuine but as only a way-station, never the destination. The destination is the buddhahood reached at journey's end, the true "ending of birth-and-death," and it turns out to be the opposite of preservation. At the Cloud-Ferry the pilgrims must cross on a bottomless boat, and as they cross they see a human corpse come floating down the current; Tripitaka recoils, and is then congratulated — the body is his own. To become a buddha is not to keep the body forever but to watch it drift away and be glad. The deepest deathlessness in the whole book lies not in cheating the register, nor in refining the flesh into something that lasts, but in setting the dying body down.

So the novel arrives, by the long road of myth, at the same place the literati reach in a handful of characters. Becker's machinery is dramatized and then judged: the immortality project, played out across every rung from theft to alchemy, is shown to fail at exactly the point where it tries to keep something. What passes the river is the one who stops trying to. It is the mirror image of Becker's diagnosis — the same craving for symbolic survival, traced all the way up its hierarchy — and at the top the craving dissolves rather than being satisfied. To know the work of creation, the couplet says, read the tale of release: and the release, in the end, is from the wish not to die.

《西游记》（[19]）在神通与插科打诨之下，只靠一台隐藏的发动机运转：其中各类存在——神、仙、妖、王——无一不在设法从死亡底下钻出来，整部一百回的大机器，都由这一桩渴望推动。回末那副对联已把话说在明处。欲知造化会元功（天地如何在邵雍式的会元大周期里生灭流转），书中便叫人去读一部《西游释厄传》，而这正是此书早年的题名之一。那要被「释」的「厄」，归根到底就是死亡；全书是一篇关于「人如何设法解除死亡」的长长考察。

它的开端，恰好落在贝克尔的论题所预言之处。美猴王是花果山上最快活的造物，果木流泉的乐园之王，直到有一天，他看着一只老猴死去，省悟到连自己也记在阎王的生死簿上。宴乐之中，他忽然落下泪来；次日清晨，便独自漂洋过海，去寻师访道。求不死，正起于死亡初次被瞥见的那一刻：不是出于悲苦，而是出于欢乐之中那突如其来、不可忍受的明白：这一切会终结。书中此后的一切奋斗，都是那第一次「逃离簿子」的延续。

随之展开的，是一个长生的等级，而每一级又被逐一敲落。最初、也最粗鄙的，是偷来的长生，靠强力或欺诈攫取。猴王偷吃了蟠桃与道家的金丹，又在被勾入幽冥时，夺过生死簿，提笔把自己与族类的名字一笔勾销，直接在簿子上作弊。这为他换来数百年寿数和天大的麻烦，而它恰恰是受惩罚的那一种：被压在五行山下五百年。取经路上那些食人的妖怪，想要的是同一样东西、走的是同一条捷径（吞食唐僧，据说其肉可致长生），它们正是「偷来」这条路的食欲形态。人无法把自己从簿子上抹去；这一企图本身就是罪。

偷窃之上，是修炼而得的长生，即猴王在山中所受的真正内丹功夫：戒律、吐纳、对自我的提炼。书中视它为真实，却只是一个中途站，从不是终点。终点是取经路尽头所证的佛果，那真正的「了生死」，而它竟是「保全」的反面。凌云渡上，师徒须乘一只无底之船而渡；正渡时，但见一具死尸顺流漂来，唐僧惊避，旋即被人恭贺：那尸首正是他自己。成佛，不是把身体永久留住，而是看着它漂走，并且为之欣然。全书最深的不死，不在于蒙骗簿册，不在于把肉身炼成经久之物，而在于把那具会死的身体放下。

于是这部小说，经由神话的漫漫长路，抵达了文人以寥寥数字所抵达的同一处。贝克尔的那台机器被搬演出来，随即被审判：不朽工程，从偷窃到炼丹、一级级演遍，正是在它试图留住某物的那一点上败下阵来。渡过那条河的，是那个不再去留的人。这是贝克尔诊断的镜像：同一种对象征性存续的渴望，被沿着它的等级一路追到顶端，而在顶端，那渴望不是被满足，而是消解。欲知造化会元功，对联说，须看那部释厄之传；而所释的，归根到底，是那「不愿死」的愿望本身。

VI · Before Old Age Comes 不知老之将至 · Before Old Age Comes

Delighting in what one meets, content for the while in oneself, one does not know that old age is on its way. … But what one delighted in, in a glance, has already become a trace. To equate life and death is a fiction; to make Peng and the short-lived one is a fabrication. Is it not grievous! Life and death are vast matters indeed. Wang Xizhi, Preface to the Orchid Pavilion Collection (353), paraphrased
当其欣于所遇，暂得于己，怡然自足，不知老之将至。……向之所欣，俯仰之间，已为陈迹。……固知一死生为虚诞，齐彭殇为妄作。岂不痛哉！死生亦大矣。 王羲之，《兰亭集序》（353）
Only the clear wind on the river and the bright moon between the hills — the ear meets them and they become sound, the eye meets them and they become colour. Take them, and there is no prohibition; use them, and they are never used up. Su Shi, First Rhapsody on the Red Cliff (1082), paraphrased
惟江上之清风，与山间之明月，耳得之而为声，目遇之而成色，取之无禁，用之不竭。 苏轼，《前赤壁赋》（1082）

Read on its own, "delighting in what one meets, one does not know that old age is on its way" looks like an ordinary line of carpe diem. Its force is in the reversal that comes right after. The wording is pointed: "content for the while" marks the contentment as borrowed time, and "does not know that old age is coming" registers not praise but a lapse of attention — the contentment depends on not looking at the old age drawing near. In the terms of this whole sequence, the line is itself a specimen of falling: to be absorbed in the joy of what one meets is to have screened mortality off.

The next lines break it open. What was delighted in a moment ago has, in the space of a glance up and down, already become a trace, and long life and short alike end in extinction — which draws the famous cry, that life and death are vast matters, and is it not grievous. And then Wang Xizhi deposes a judge, exactly as Kundera will do thirteen centuries later [16]. The judge he deposes is Zhuangzi [18]. "To equate life and death is a fiction; to make Peng and the short-lived one is a fabrication" — to treat living and dying as one thing, to set the long-lived and the early-dead on a level, is, he says flatly, a fiction and a fabrication. The line cuts straight against the Daoist mysticism fashionable among the gentry of his day, the Zhuangzi-derived anaesthetic that dissolves the pain of death by equalizing death with life. Wang Xizhi will not take the anaesthetic. He would rather keep the grief than accept a transcendence whose whole function is to make the grief go away.

That refusal of cheap comfort is the thread running through every position gathered here. Schopenhauer's denial of the will [9], the Buddhist ending of birth-and-death, Zhuangzi's equalizing of life and death — each is, in its way, a device for cancelling the pain of dying; while Heidegger's bearing, Cervantes' leaning-toward-rashness, and Kundera's deposed judge each keep the pain and refuse the comfort. Wang Xizhi, in 353, in a handful of characters, comes down on the side that keeps it. What he offers in place of consolation is to set down the people who were present and record what they said, so that those who come after will, in his phrase, likewise be moved by these words — the eastern counterpart of Kundera's depreciated legacy of Cervantes. Not immortality, which he has just denied in refusing to equate Peng with the short-lived; not an afterlife; but writing, transmission, a thing turned toward later readers that asks nothing in return, is itself destined to become a trace, and is set down all the same.

Su Shi, seven centuries later, takes up the question where Wang Xizhi leaves it [17]. Wang Xizhi stops at the grief, at "is it not grievous," having faced the fact without setting it down, so that grief stays the keynote. In the rhapsody a guest is lamenting how brief a life is, wishing he could "soar with the immortals and embrace the bright moon to the end of time," and finding it impossible — one more longing for immortality. Su Shi answers first by separating the changing from the unchanging: what passes is like this river, yet it has never gone; what waxes and wanes is like that moon, yet in the end it neither shrinks nor grows. Seen from the side of the unchanging, thing and self are alike inexhaustible, and there is nothing in the river to envy. The anxiety of finitude is cancelled — not by an anaesthetic, but by a change of vantage.

Then comes the landing. The clear wind on the river and the bright moon between the hills may be taken with no prohibition and used without ever being used up, because one does not own them at all; one only receives them. It is the same gesture as the cup of the Rubáiyát, but lighter and cheaper — the cup has to be filled and will be drained and is a form of possession, whereas the wind and the moon need no owning. The ear meets them and they are sound; the eye meets them and they are colour. It is the lightest version of honouring the present, the one in which even the effort of honouring is set down and only the meeting is left. And Su Shi clears the Rubáiyát on the one point that has held across all of this: he does not use the comfort to cancel the pain. The guest is allowed to grieve the whole way through, and only afterward, on the far side of the grief, are the wind and the moon handed over — so the ease is not evasion but the thing reached by passing through. It holds what Wang Xizhi could not set down. Wang Xizhi stops at the grief; Su Shi grants that the grief is real, and adds only that once it is spent, the wind is still blowing and the moon is still bright, and that the two of them ask no price at all.

单独读，「当其欣于所遇……不知老之将至」像一句寻常的及时行乐。它的力量，在紧随其后的反转里。这措辞是有所指的：「暂得于己」把那份自足标记为借来的时光，而「不知老之将至」所记的并非赞许，而是一次注意力的失守：那份自足，正以不去看那正逼近的「老」为前提。放在这一整条线索里看，这一句本身就是沉沦的一个标本——沉浸于所遇之乐，恰恰就是把必死遮蔽掉了。

下面几句便把它掀开。方才所欣，俯仰之间，已成陈迹，而修短随化、终归于尽；由此引出那著名的一叹：死生亦大矣，岂不痛哉。而后，王羲之废黜了一个审判者，恰如十三个世纪之后昆德拉将要做的那样[16]。他废黜的审判者，是庄子[18]。「固知一死生为虚诞，齐彭殇为妄作」——把生与死当作一回事、把长寿者与夭折者摆在同一水平上，他平直地说，是虚诞，是妄作。这一句直冲他那个时代士族间盛行的玄学而去，冲着那源出庄子、以「齐生死」消解死亡之痛的麻药。王羲之不肯服这剂麻药。他宁可留着那悲痛，也不接受一种全部功能只在于消去悲痛的超越。

这种对廉价安慰的拒绝，是贯穿此处所辑每一个立场的那根线。叔本华的否定意志[9]、佛教的了生死、庄子的齐生死，各自以其方式，都是取消死亡之痛的装置；而海德格尔的承担、塞万提斯的偏向鲁莽、昆德拉的废黜审判者，则各自留着那痛，拒绝那安慰。王羲之，在公元 353 年，以寥寥数字，落在了留着它的那一边。他在安慰之外所给出的，是列叙在场的人、记录他们所说的话，使后来者，用他的话说，亦将有感于斯文。这正是昆德拉「塞万提斯被贬低的传承」的东方对应。不是长生，他刚刚在拒绝「齐彭殇」时否定了它；不是来世；而是文字，是传承，是一个朝向后来读者、分文不索、本身也终将成为陈迹、却依然被写下的东西。

七个世纪之后，苏轼在王羲之止步之处接起这个问题[17]。王羲之停在悲痛上，停在「岂不痛哉」，他直面了那事实，却没有把它放下，于是悲痛始终是基调。赋中有客，正哀叹人生何其短促，恨不能「挟飞仙以遨游，抱明月而长终」，却知其不可得，又是一次对长生的渴望。苏轼先以变与不变作答：逝者如斯，而未尝往也；盈虚者如彼，而卒莫消长也。自不变者观之，则物与我皆无尽，长江之中并无可羡。有限的焦虑由此被取消：不是靠麻药，而是靠视角的一次转换。

而后是那落点。江上之清风、山间之明月，取之无禁、用之不竭，因为人根本不曾拥有它们，只是领受它们。这与《鲁拜集》的那杯酒是同一个手势，却更轻、更省：那杯酒须被斟满、终将饮尽，是一种占有，而清风与明月无须占有。耳遇之则成声，目遇之则成色。这是敬重当下最轻盈的形态，连「敬」的那份用力都被放下，所余者唯有相遇。而苏轼在那贯穿这一切的一点上，越过了《鲁拜集》：他不以安慰来取消那痛。客得以把悲痛走完整整一程，唯有在那之后，在悲痛的另一侧，清风与明月才被递出来：于是那份释然不是回避，而是穿越之后所抵达之物。它承住了王羲之所不能放下者。王羲之停在悲痛上；苏轼则承认那悲痛为真，只补上一句：悲痛一旦走尽，风仍在吹，月仍在明，而这两者，分文不索。

死亡这一漠然的事实，与对它惯常的遗忘；对几种回避它的手段的逐一拆解，经由未来，经由不朽工程，经由那个焦虑的明日；「宁取过度的行动，而非怯懦的缺席」这一取向；废黜一切外部审判者，转而归依于一个分文不索的传承；美猴王那座长生的等级，在顶端坍缩为「把身体放下」；王羲之对「直面事实、拒绝虚假安慰」的坚持；以及苏轼，在悲痛的另一侧，对耳目所遇之清风明月的陈述。诸般立场，自对有限的认识，一路行至对当下的领受，取材于叔本华、贝克尔、海德格尔，以及波斯、西班牙、捷克与中国的来源，最终汇于公元 1082 年一个被贬黄州之人所写的两句。

The indifferent fact of death and the habit of forgetting it; the dismantling, one by one, of the means of evading it — through the future, through the immortality project, through the anxious tomorrow; the preference for acting in excess over a cowardly absence; the deposition of every external judge in favour of a legacy that asks no return; the Monkey King's hierarchy of deathlessness collapsing, at its summit, into setting the body down; Wang Xizhi's insistence on facing the fact and refusing false comfort; and Su Shi, on the far side of grief, on the clear wind and bright moon met by ear and eye. The positions run from the recognition of finitude to the reception of the present, drawing on Schopenhauer, Becker, Heidegger, and the Persian, Spanish, Czech, and Chinese sources, and converging on two lines written in 1082 by a man exiled to Huangzhou.
